## Rollout Principle

Do not sell a revolution. Announce an apparatus.

The safe public sentence:

> Sundog is a traceability harness for indirect-inference alignment: a way to
> test whether a system can act from indirect signals, whether the route is
> inspectable, and where the coupling fails.

The founder-voice sentence:

> We started with a wild "shadow becomes signal" intuition, then spent a year
> trying to break it.

The credibility sentence:

> The strongest thing we can say is not that Sundog is universal. It is that
> the repo now contains small, inspectable systems, explicit baselines, and
> named failure boundaries.

> **FORE. The load-bearing postulate we are actually chasing.**
>
> The Coarse-Graining Postulate, now staged in its own roadmap. A controller
> does not need to invert the world; it needs the signature to be a
> *sufficient statistic for the optimal action* — Blackwell sufficiency with
> respect to the control objective, not state reconstruction. Rate-distortion
> reading via the Shannon seed. Provable in toy LQG and finite-MDP cases
> where both Φ and π* are known; the falsifier is explicit. A task where the
> optimal policy is provably 𝓕_σ-measurable yet signature-only control
> fails to reach Bayes-optimal cost on that set kills it; the converse on
> the pushable-occluder boundary kills it too.
>
> Two corollaries I want to name as conjectures, not results:
>
> - Goodhart-immunity reframed as capacity-relative one-wayness of the
>   reward channel. The agent can only game what it can invert; "immunity"
>   is a relation between Φ and the agent's capacity class, not an
>   attribute of the agent. The Mesa cliff is *predicted* by this reading
>   rather than contradicting it — and it predicts the cliff location moves
>   with capacity. Falsifiable.
> - The 16x slowdown treated as a candidate conserved quantity rather than
>   an embarrassment: (information withheld from the observation) × (excess
>   acquisition time) ≥ const. Predicts the slowdown from the
>   dimensionality gap across substrates. If the ratio is not conserved,
>   the conjecture dies.

## Response Bank

**"Is this just inverse problems are hard?"**

Mostly yes, and that is the useful correction. The claim is not that inversion
is easy. It is that some signatures are sufficient for control even when they
are not sufficient for reconstruction.

**"Did you solve alignment?"**

No. The project is about traceable evaluation harnesses for systems acting
under partial observability.

**"Does the agent actually use the inferred variable?"**

That is exactly the hard question. Probe decodability is not enough. The next
traceability test has to be causal and structural: counterfactual steerability
plus failure-boundary coincidence.

**"Why should anyone care about shadows?"**

Because many real systems cannot access the true state directly. They get
traces: light, force, pressure, deformation, local fields, behavior. Sundog
asks when those traces are enough to act, and where they stop being enough.

**"Why so many demos?"**

The demos are not proofs. They are workbenches: small places where the same
pattern can be measured, falsified, or demoted.
