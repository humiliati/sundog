Kakeya finite-field reader & workbench

A boundary-correctness review surface, not a result. We built a plain-language reader of Dvir's 2008 finite-field Kakeya theorem in a "body / shadow" vocabulary, a tiny F_q² workbench, and a graphic that places Kakeya at the exact-maximal end of a cross-substrate "body-resistance" axis. The ask to a reviewer is narrow: does the explainer teach the right boundary, or does any of it overreach?

What this is NOT.
  • Not a Kakeya result — Sundog has none.
  • Not a finite-field → Euclidean transfer, and nothing about the open Euclidean problem.
  • Not a claim that Wang–Zahl's 2025 R³ result bears on open n ≥ 4.
  • Not a "regime-2 / control-sufficiency" witness — explicitly fenced (see Q3).

The artifact

  • Reader — Dvir's theorem (every Kakeya set in F_q^n has ≥ C_n q^n points) walked in body/shadow vocabulary: the complete direction-shadow forces a low-degree polynomial certificate to vanish everywhere — contradiction.
  • Tiny F_q² workbench (q ∈ {5, 7, 11}) — toggle points, watch direction coverage; the lesson is that direction-completeness forces a constant fraction of the plane, not a vanishing set.
  • Body-resistance gallery — the cross-substrate placement graphic.

The review ask (five questions)

Full text in the external review packet; the headline three:

  • Q1 — exposition fidelity. Is the body/shadow retelling of Dvir faithful, and is the displayed floor C(q+1, 2) the right proof bound to show (and clearly not the exact planar minimum)?
  • Q2 — boundary discipline. Are the four registers kept separate — finite-field (Dvir) / Euclidean R³ (Wang–Zahl) / open n ≥ 4 / toy — and is the set-vs-maximal-function distinction handled right?
  • Q3 — the body-resistance fence (the one we most want challenged). We read Kakeya as body-resistance and explicitly deny it is a "control-sufficiency / regime-2" separation, because the direction-shadow is control-sufficient only trivially (it is the direction). Is that fence correct and strong enough?
