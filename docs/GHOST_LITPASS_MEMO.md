## Purpose

Move Ghost from a strong hook into a bounded reader lane:

> Draw the circle. Find the ghost.

The usable technical spine is:

> A finite inside can be locally lawful without being globally closed. The
> "ghost" is the outside dependency that makes the inside meaningful.

Aperiodic tiling is the first hard substrate because it gives an unusually
clean instance of local rules producing global non-periodic order. Engineering
adds a second substrate: modules, runtimes, compilers, trust bases, and proof
checkers all depend on assumptions outside their local source text. Philosophy
adds the guardrail vocabulary: self-reference, metalanguage, map/territory,
and standpoint.

This memo's job is not to prove a new principle. It is to identify which
citations can carry weight if Ghost becomes a reader, workbench, or essay lane,
and to make theorem-shaped expectations exit before they harden.

## Claim Boundary

Sundog did not prove, verify, extend, or formalize any tiling, logic,
engineering, or philosophy result.

This lane explicitly does not claim:

1. a new aperiodic monotile;
2. a proof of a new tiling theorem;
3. a formal invariant called "outside debt";
4. that every aperiodic tiling is hierarchical;
5. that finite-patch ambiguity is new;
6. that the Hat or Spectre proves a general inside/outside principle;
7. that Godel's incompleteness theorem literally says "nothing inside a circle
   can explain itself";
8. that software modules, proof assistants, or compilers are equivalent to
   formal systems;
9. that "ghost" is more than Sundog vocabulary until mapped onto existing
   mathematical or engineering terms.

The admissible claim is narrower:

1. Aperiodic tilings are a defensible reader/workbench substrate for the
   local-rule/global-order tension.
2. Existing tiling vocabulary already names much of the terrain: hulls, local
   topology, finite local complexity, repetitivity, recognizability, mutual
   local derivability, matching rules, substitution, cut-and-project, and
   shifts of finite type.
3. "Ghost" can be used as public-facing language for boundary dependency only
   if the technical document always names the existing formal object underneath.
4. Engineering analogies are useful only as analogies unless a concrete
   checker, runtime, trust base, or attack model is specified.

## Q2 Resolution - Outside Debt Maps to Existing Objects

**Added 2026-06-27.** Resolves Lit-Pass Question 2 (and Follow-Up Ratchet 7):
is "outside debt" just recognizability, repetitivity, local derivability, MLD,
cohomology, hull structure, or extension complexity in disguise? Short answer:
**yes - it is not a new invariant.** It decomposes onto existing objects, and
which one applies depends on the substrate.

Definitions locked to source:

- **Local derivability (radius r):** a tiling T is locally derivable from S if
  there is a radius r such that whenever S agrees on the r-ball around x and the
  r-ball around y up to translation, T agrees at x and y. **Mutual local
  derivability (MLD)** is local derivability in both directions, and is an
  equivalence relation. Origin: M. Baake, M. Schlottmann, P. D. Jarvis,
  "Quasiperiodic tilings with tenfold symmetry and equivalence with respect to
  local derivability," J. Phys. A: Math. Gen. 24 (1991) 4637-4654; definition
  via the Bielefeld Tilings Encyclopedia,
  https://tilings.math.uni-bielefeld.de/glossary/mld/.
- **Recognizability = unique composition property:** a finite radius R such that
  the level-1 supertile decomposition around any point is determined by the
  radius-R patch - the substitution can be locally undone. Mosse (1992) for the
  symbolic case; Solomyak proved for self-affine translationally finite tilings
  that unique composition is equivalent to nonperiodicity (Boris Solomyak,
  "Nonperiodicity implies unique composition for self-similar translationally
  finite tilings," Discrete Comput. Geom. 20 (1998) 265-279,
  https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/PL00009386).
- **Hull cohomology:** Cech / pattern-equivariant cohomology of the tiling space
  is an MLD-invariant of the whole class (Kellendonk-Putnam; Lorenzo Sadun,
  *Topology of Tiling Spaces*, AMS University Lecture Series 46, 2008).

The mapping:

| "Outside debt" sense | Existing object it already is |
| --- | --- |
| how much patch is needed to recover your supertile (ghost-as-ancestry) | recognizability radius / unique composition property |
| how much patch to translate between two descriptions (Hat / CAP / rhombic / cut-and-project) | local-derivability radius / MLD |
| what is invariant across the whole class (the "outside" of the entire hull) | Cech / pattern-equivariant cohomology |
| order vs randomness, recurrence | patch-complexity function p(r), repetitivity - these measure growth, not debt |
| "given a patch, how much outside is needed to extend at all" | extension / decision complexity (Domino Problem; undecidable in general) |

Vocabulary consequence: keep "outside debt" as public umbrella language only.
Any technical note must name which of {recognizability radius, local-derivability
radius, cohomology, extension complexity} is meant.

**Two-regime finding (the load-bearing part).** These objects do not lie on one
axis. They split at a phase boundary:

- **Bounded regime (substitution / hierarchical, including Hat/Spectre).** By
  Mosse-Solomyak, nonperiodicity is exactly what makes the supertile ancestry
  *locally recoverable at a finite radius*. The ghost here is finite and
  measurable - the recognizability radius. The outside is *bounded*, not
  unerasable.
- **Unbounded regime (general local rules / SFT / Wang).** No finite radius
  exists; the relevant quantity is the extension problem, which is undecidable in
  general (Berger 1966; Robinson 1971; cf. the 2025 tromino result). This is a
  computability wall, not a recognizability radius.

This corrects the lane's central metaphor: "geometry with an unerasable outside"
holds only in the unbounded regime. For the Hat/Spectre anchors the outside is
bounded and recoverable, which is the *better* and more honest story. The
"Outside Debt Gradient" ladder in `SUNDOG_V_GHOST.md` therefore conflated two
regimes; it has been split at the phase boundary, and the aperiodic-monotile row
relabeled from "unbounded non-periodic context" to a finite recognizability
radius.

Metric implication (informs Ratchet 3): **recognizability radius is the
recommended first metric.** It is the only candidate that is simultaneously
finite, classically defined (the unique composition property), and directly
visualizable as the ghost collapsing as the window grows. Local-derivability
radius is the natural second metric once more than one description is on screen.

## Track A - Local Rules and Global Order

The safest mathematical phrasing is:

> Local rules can force global order that is not periodic.

This is not Sundog-original. It is the core aperiodic-tiling story from Wang,
Berger, Robinson, Penrose, Goodman-Strauss, and Hat/Spectre.

Technical vocabulary to prefer over loose Ghost language:

| Sundog phrase | Existing vocabulary to check first |
| --- | --- |
| circle | finite patch, ball, window, local pattern |
| outside | extension, hull, local topology, boundary condition |
| ghost | unresolved continuation, hierarchy, ancestry, factor, transversal data |
| hidden larger order | substitution structure, matching-rule-enforced hierarchy, cut-and-project data |
| outside debt | recognizability radius, local derivability radius, extension complexity, patch complexity, repetitivity |
| closure | periodic point / period lattice / fundamental domain / finite generator |

The Ghost workbench should make these terms visible rather than inventing a
parallel folklore. Public copy can say "ghost"; technical notes should say
which formal object is being visualized.

## Track B - Hat/Spectre as the First Hard Substrate

Hat and Spectre are the right first public anchors because they are recent,
visually memorable, and mathematically real.

The useful proof-shape summary:

1. The local shape admits tilings of the plane.
2. Its tilings organize into metatiles or substitution/hierarchical structure.
3. The hierarchy blocks translational periodicity.
4. Later work studies the associated hulls, topology, spectra, and related
   constructions.

The Sundog sentence that survives the literature pass:

> In Hat/Spectre-style aperiodicity, the finite patch is not meaningless or
> random; it is a local view into a forced larger structure.

Hard fences:

- Do not imply the Hat and Spectre are the only or final form of aperiodicity.
- Do not imply all aperiodic tile systems admit clean substitution ancestry.
- Do not reuse figures, tile outlines, or generated patches without checking
  license/source discipline.
- Do not call "the ghost" a proof ingredient unless it is mapped to metatiles,
  substitution, recognizability, hulls, or another named object.

## Track C - Undecidability and Extension

The Domino Problem is the hard wall behind this lane.

Berger and Robinson mean:

> Some local tiling systems have extension/global-existence questions that are
> undecidable.

For Sundog, this matters because it prevents the naive version of the Ghost
Boundary Principle:

> "Given a finite patch, measure the outside it needs."

That sounds innocent, but the general extension problem can encode computation.
So the workbench must be substrate-specific. It may compare carefully chosen
systems; it may not promise a universal finite-patch oracle.

Admissible workbench tiers should be split, not ranked as a smooth gradient.

Bounded reader substrates:

1. **Periodic controls:** repeat cell exists; ghost collapses when enough of the
   cell is captured.
2. **Substitution controls:** hierarchy is recoverable or partially recoverable;
   ghost becomes ancestry/recognizability.
3. **Cut-and-project controls:** outside is projection/window/phase data.

Computability cliff:

4. **SFT/Wang edge cases:** extension can be computationally hard or undecidable;
   ghost may not be visualizable as a friendly hierarchy.

This is one of the most important claim-boundary outcomes of the pass. The
Domino-Problem result should appear in the public/exhibit planning as a cliff,
not as the bottom rung of an "outside debt gradient." Otherwise the reader lane
silently promises a scalar measure where the literature gives a hard
computability boundary.

## Track D - Physical and Material Order

> Ordered does not mean periodic.

Recent 2025-2026 application papers show aperiodic order continuing to matter
in materials, kirigami/metamaterials, moire-inspired construction, percolation,

- Use quasicrystals as philosophical and visual support.
- Do not claim Ghost predicts, designs, or explains new materials.
- If the lane later becomes a physical-design page, split it into a new memo;
  the math-reader lane and materials-engineering lane have different standards.

## Candidate Metric Family

Do not call any of these "the Ghost invariant" yet.

**Repeat-cell capture radius**

For periodic controls, the smallest finite region needed to infer/certify a
period lattice within a chosen pattern family.

Use: shows ghost collapse.

**Recognizability radius**

For substitution systems, the local radius needed to uniquely recover supertile
decomposition.

Use: best current candidate for "ghost as hidden ancestry."

**Local derivability radius**

For two tiling descriptions, the radius needed to derive one local pattern from
the other.

Use: useful if comparing Hat, CAP, rhombic, or cut-and-project descriptions.

**Continuation count**

For a finite patch under local rules, count or sample legal extensions to a
larger box.

Use: good visual intuition.

Risk: can explode, be boundary-dominated, or become undecidable in general.

**Patch complexity / repetitivity**

Count distinct patches up to translation as a function of radius, or measure how
far one must search to see repeats.

Use: distinguishes order from randomness/repetition.

Risk: may be mathematically standard enough that "ghost" adds no value.

**Hull periodic-point check**

Ask whether the tiling hull contains periodic tilings, not merely whether one
displayed tiling is non-periodic.

Use: protects terminology. A single non-periodic tiling is not the same as an
aperiodic tile system.

  an explicit hierarchy.

> Ordered does not mean repeating. Local does not mean closed.
