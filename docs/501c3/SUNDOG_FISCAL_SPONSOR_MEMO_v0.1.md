**Version:** 0.1

Governance and Claims-Review Policy).
**Companion to:** *Founding Plan v0.1* §6, month 1–3 milestone.
**Date:** 2026-05-21

---

## 0. Purpose

The Founding Plan commits the lab to having a fiscal sponsor in
place by month 3. This memo scopes that decision: it lists the
criteria that matter for *this* lab, evaluates four candidate
sponsors plus a university-partnership placeholder, names one
sponsor that has been ruled out, and ends with a provisional
recommendation.

The memo's job is **to scope the decision, not to make it**. Every
factual claim about a specific sponsor is tagged *Hypothesised*.
Founders are expected to verify these in direct conversation with
each sponsor before signing.

---

## 1. Decision criteria

What matters for the lab's first year:

- **Overhead / fee structure.** Percentage of revenue, flat fees,
  or hybrid. Lower is better only insofar as it doesn't cost
  capacity that matters elsewhere.
- **AI-policy compatibility.** Whether the sponsor's institutional
  position is compatible with public-interest evaluation of AI
  deployments and with publishing claim-tagged research on
  third-party systems. This is the criterion most likely to
  surprise.
- **Grant-management capacity.** Whether the sponsor can receive
  and administer grants from AI-safety, public-interest, and
  scientific-research funders. A sponsor without grant-management
  capacity is not useful given the lab's grant-first funding
  theory (Founding Plan §10).
- **Transition support.** How cleanly the lab can exit to direct
  501(c)(3): IP retention, donor history, contract assignment,
  bank-account transition.
- **Speed to onboard.** Weeks (PPF, HCB) vs. months (CS&S, Tides).
  Onboarding speed bounds when first grants can be applied for.
- **Project-size fit.** Some sponsors have minimum scale, some
  cap maximum scale.
- **Reputation / legitimacy.** Whether sponsorship helps or
  hinders credibility with academic funders, AI-safety foundations,
  and potential evaluation partners.

These criteria are tagged *Normative* — the weighting is a values
judgment specific to the lab's first-year posture.

## 2. Candidates

All facts in this section are tagged *Hypothesised* and require
founder verification. The memo is intended to bound the search, not
to substitute for the sponsor's own onboarding materials.

### 2.1 Players Philanthropy Fund (PPF)

- **Type.** General-purpose 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsor.
- **Overhead.** Low (often cited at ~5% of revenue; verify).
- **AI compatibility.** General-purpose; no specific AI-research
  policy known. Likely permissive, but the lab's evaluation-of-
  third-party-systems posture should be raised explicitly in the
  intake conversation — sponsors sometimes have liability concerns
  about projects that publicly evaluate other parties.
- **Grant access.** Yes, can receive grants on behalf of project.
- **Transition.** Generally clean.
- **Speed.** Weeks.
- **Pros.** Low overhead, fast onboarding, accepts diverse
  projects.
- **Cons.** General-purpose; the institutional brand does not
  signal "research lab." Academic funders may not recognise it.

### 2.2 Hack Club Bank (HCB) — under The Hack Foundation

- **Type.** Newer fiscal sponsor; tech-native infrastructure.
- **Overhead.** Low (often cited at ~7%; verify).
- **AI compatibility.** Tech-friendly; has sponsored
  AI-adjacent projects. Verify that AI-evaluation research is
  in-scope and not borderline-disallowed.
- **Grant access.** Yes; integrated with their banking platform.
- **Transition.** Reasonably clean.
- **Speed.** Days to weeks; the fastest option among the
  candidates.
- **Pros.** Tech-native, fastest onboarding, strong engineering
  infrastructure for budget and expense tracking.
- **Cons.** Younger fiscal-sponsorship operation; some academic
  and government funders may not recognise it; communication style
  is informal and may not match the lab's public-interest posture.

### 2.3 Code for Science & Society (CS&S)

- **Type.** Public-interest research-focused fiscal sponsor.
- **Overhead.** Higher (often cited in the 10–15% range; verify).
- **AI compatibility.** Research-oriented; receptive to
  public-interest evaluation work in principle, but the lab's
  *evaluation-of-third-party-AI-systems* posture should be raised
  explicitly because it is not typical research-org scope.
- **Grant access.** Strong grant-management capacity, particularly
  with scientific-research funders.
- **Transition.** Documented pathway to spin out as standalone
  501(c)(3).
- **Speed.** Months. The intake process is substantive.
- **Pros.** Best institutional fit for a research-lab brand;
  legitimacy with academic and foundation funders; mission
  alignment with public-interest research.
- **Cons.** Higher overhead; slower onboarding; the substantive
  intake process means the first grant cycle may be missed.

### 2.4 Tides Center (or comparable large sponsor)

- **Type.** Large, established public-interest fiscal sponsor.
- **Overhead.** 10%+ commonly; higher for complex or unusual
  projects.
- **AI compatibility.** Sponsors AI-adjacent advocacy and research
  work; verify scope.
- **Grant access.** Established grant infrastructure.
- **Transition.** Possible but bureaucratic.
- **Speed.** Months.
- **Pros.** Established legitimacy; institutional infrastructure;
  strong with foundation funders.
- **Cons.** Highest overhead; corporate posture; not tech-native;
  bureaucratic operational style.

### 2.5 University-based partnership (placeholder)

- **Type.** A university-affiliated centre for AI, ethics, or
  public-interest research sponsors the lab as a hosted project.
  Specifics depend entirely on founders' connections and on the
  university's policies.
- **Pros.** Academic credibility; potential research-grant
  pipeline; access to graduate students and faculty advisors.
- **Cons.** University IP and publication-control policies may
  conflict with the lab's open-publication discipline. Some
  universities claim institutional rights in research outputs of
  hosted projects. Transitioning out can require negotiation
  over the lab's name, brand, and IP. The lab's
  policy-mandated open claims log may be hard to reconcile with
  some universities' pre-publication review requirements.
- **Action.** Worth pursuing **only if** a specific institutional
  partner is identified whose IP and publication policies have
  been read and accepted. Not a generic fallback.

### Ruled out: Open Collective Foundation (OCF)

OCF announced wind-down of its US fiscal-sponsorship programme in
2024 and is, on the best information available to this memo, no
longer accepting new fiscally sponsored projects. Tagged
*Hypothesised pending verification* because policies change; if a
founder has a personal contact at OCF or hears otherwise, the
memo can be revised.

The Open Collective *platform* (Open Collective Inc.) is distinct
from OCF and does not by itself provide fiscal sponsorship.

## 3. Provisional recommendation

This section is tagged *Normative*.

**Highest-fit short-list, in priority order:**

- **Code for Science & Society.** Best institutional fit for a
  research-lab brand. Risk: slower onboarding may delay the first
  grant cycle by 1–2 quarters.
- **Players Philanthropy Fund.** Best fee structure and fastest
  operational path. Risk: less brand fit for academic funders.

**Recommended approach:** open conversations with **both** in
parallel during month 1. Submit a CS&S application as the
preferred path; keep PPF as the fast-fallback if CS&S declines, is
slow, or imposes evaluation-scope restrictions the lab cannot
accept.

**HCB** is a strong third candidate if speed is the binding
constraint — for example if a specific grant deadline in month 2–3
forces the issue. It is unlikely to be the right long-term home,
but it can be a "month-1-to-month-9 bridge" if necessary.

**Tides** is not recommended for v0.1 unless a specific foundation
relationship makes the higher overhead worthwhile. Revisit if
larger grants are in play later.

**University partnership** is not recommended generically; pursue
only if a specific partner with compatible IP and publication
policies is identified.

## 4. What founders must verify before committing

Every factual claim about specific sponsors in §2 is *Hypothesised*
and must be confirmed in writing with the sponsor before
commitment. Specifically:

- Current overhead percentage and any unit-of-account variation
  (revenue, donations only, grants only, in-kind).
- The sponsor's policy on the lab's two distinctive activities:
  (a) publishing public-interest evaluations of third-party AI
  deployments, and (b) publishing the framework documents
  (Initiative, Policy, harness whitepaper) under a permissive
  open licence.
- Grant-management capacity for the specific funders the lab
  intends to apply to.
- Transition pathway to direct 501(c)(3): contract assignment,
  donor history, IP, branding.
- Pre-publication review requirements, if any.
- Communications and branding restrictions — whether the lab can
  publish under "Sundog Research Lab" while sponsored.
- Conflict-of-interest constraints relevant to policy §8.
- Constraints on paid evaluation engagements (Founding Plan §10
  treats this as last-priority funding, but the sponsor's policy
  may rule it in or out entirely).
- Termination and exit terms.

A short standard intake form covering these questions, sent to
each shortlisted sponsor, accelerates the decision.

## 5. Decision timeline

Per Founding Plan §6:

| Month  | Action                                                          |
|--------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|
| 0–1    | Shortlist 2–3 candidates from §3. Prepare intake-question form. |
| 1–2    | Initial meetings with each shortlisted sponsor. Verify §4 items.|
| 2      | Submit application(s) to preferred sponsor(s).                  |
| 3      | Agreement signed; fiscal sponsorship operational.               |

The lab's first grant applications may be drafted in parallel with
sponsor selection, but cannot be submitted until the sponsor
agreement is signed.
