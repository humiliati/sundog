## 1. Purpose

This document drafts the **Defensive Patent Pledge** that the IP
strategy memo §10 second open issue flagged as worth a clause in the
Governance Policy. It resolves that open issue toward a concrete
proposal so that the §6 sequencing question (file / defer / decline)
can be discussed against a known pledge posture rather than a
hypothetical one.

The substantive choice this draft commits to: a **defensive-only**
patent posture, modeled on the Twitter Innovator's Patent Agreement
(IPA, 2012) for inventor-veto structure, the LOT Network for
transferee binding, and the Tesla 2014 pledge for the broad
public-commitment framing — with a Sundog-specific scope tied to the
**open AI evaluation ecosystem**.

The pledge's compatibility with the Halo Initiative's meta-principle
is the load-bearing reason to adopt it: a lab whose framework asks
evaluated systems to bind their own commercial leverage cannot,
without contradiction, refuse to bind its own. The pledge is the
meta-principle applied to the Lab's potential patent portfolio.

---

## 2. Posture statement — *Normative*

Sundog Research Lab adopts a **defensive-only patent posture**. The
Lab files for, prosecutes, and maintains patents *only* to the extent
necessary to (i) preserve the Lab's freedom to operate in its
research domain, (ii) deter assertion of patents against the open AI
evaluation ecosystem by hostile parties, and (iii) preserve options
for the lab's incorporated successor entities.

The Lab does not assert patents offensively, does not license patents
for revenue, and does not transfer patents to parties unwilling to
accept this same posture. The pledge in §3 makes this binding.

This posture is compatible with the lab's voluntary-framework
discipline. It is incompatible with venture-funded growth strategies
that depend on patent-based moats or licensing revenue. The Lab does
not pursue such strategies.

## 3. The Pledge — *Normative; load-bearing on adoption*

The following text is the pledge as it would read when adopted. The
square-bracketed terms reference §4 definitions. Once ratified, this
language becomes operative as §11 of the Governance Policy.

> ### Sundog Research Lab Defensive Patent Pledge
>
> **§11.1.** Sundog Research Lab pledges that it will not assert any
> [Covered Patent] against any party except in [Defensive Use], and
> will not transfer any Covered Patent to any party that has not
> accepted this Pledge in writing.
>
> **§11.2.** This Pledge applies to every patent application filed
> by the Lab, every patent granted to the Lab, and every patent the
> Lab acquires by assignment, transfer, or otherwise, from the date
> of this Pledge's adoption forward.
>
> **§11.3.** "Defensive Use" means: (a) a counterclaim or
> countersuit asserted by the Lab in response to a patent
> infringement action first brought against the Lab or against a
> party building or operating within the [Open AI Evaluation
> Ecosystem]; (b) assertion against an entity that has, in the
> twelve months preceding the assertion, filed or threatened a
> patent infringement action against the Lab, against a Lab member
> in their Lab capacity, or against any Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem
> party; or (c) assertion against a [Patent Assertion Entity] as
> defined in §11.7.
>
> **§11.4.** The Lab will not assert any Covered Patent against any
> party for use of the patented technology in good-faith research,
> publication, or building of evaluation tooling within the Open AI
> Evaluation Ecosystem, regardless of whether that party is
> commercial, academic, governmental, or otherwise organised, and
> regardless of whether their use is for profit.
>
> **§11.5.** Any transfer of a Covered Patent by the Lab (whether by
> sale, assignment, donation, contribution to a patent pool, merger,
> dissolution, or otherwise) is conditional on the transferee
> accepting this Pledge in writing as binding on the transferee and
> on all subsequent transferees. A transfer that does not satisfy
> this condition is void. This provision survives the dissolution of
> the Lab and binds any successor entity.
>
> **§11.6.** The Lab publishes annually a complete list of Covered
> Patents, including application status, grant status, jurisdiction,
> claim summary, and pledge status. The list is published as an
> appendix to the Lab's annual transparency report and is
> independently auditable.
>
> **§11.7.** "Patent Assertion Entity" means a party that derives, or
> intends to derive, the substantial part of its revenue from
> patent licensing or patent enforcement rather than from making,
> using, or selling products or providing services covered by its
> patents. The Lab maintains a publicly accessible interpretation
> note describing how this definition is applied in marginal cases.
>
> **§11.8.** "Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem" means: parties building,
> operating, or contributing in good faith to AI evaluation tooling,
> evaluation frameworks, evaluation harnesses, evaluation reports,
> or evaluation methodology, where the work is published or
> otherwise made available for public inspection. Membership in this
> ecosystem is a matter of conduct, not of formal affiliation; the
> Lab maintains a publicly accessible interpretation note describing
> how this definition is applied in marginal cases.
>
> **§11.9.** The Lab may amend this Pledge only by published
> amendment under the Governance Policy §11 versioning procedure,
> with a public-comment period of no less than 60 days, and the
> amendment may not weaken the Pledge's binding effect on patents
> covered before the amendment's effective date. Strengthening
> amendments take effect on the publication date stated in the
> amendment.
>
> **§11.10.** Each named inventor on any Covered Patent retains a
> **veto** over any assertion of that patent against any party,
> exercisable in writing to the Lab's claims-review mailbox. A
> veto stands unless overridden by a unanimous vote of the
> independent board (per Governance Policy §10) finding that
> assertion is required to preserve the Lab's existence or to
> protect the Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem from a direct attack
> against which no other remedy is adequate. Inventor vetoes and
> any overrides are published in the claims log per §5 of the
> Governance Policy.
>
> **§11.11.** This Pledge is intended as a binding, enforceable
> commitment running to the benefit of every party that would
> otherwise be exposed to assertion of a Covered Patent. Such parties
> are intended third-party beneficiaries of this Pledge to the
> maximum extent allowed by applicable law in their jurisdiction.
>
> **§11.12.** This Pledge does not waive any defense, immunity, or
> remedy otherwise available to the Lab in defending against a
> patent infringement action first brought against it. The Pledge
> binds the Lab's offensive use of patents; it does not bind the
> Lab's defense against others' offensive use.

## 4. Defined terms summary

For ease of reference, the operative defined terms in §3 are:

- **Covered Patent** — any patent application, granted patent, or
  patent acquired by the Lab from the Pledge's adoption forward.
- **Defensive Use** — the three carve-outs in §11.3.
- **Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem** — good-faith publicly-inspectable
  AI evaluation work, per §11.8.
- **Patent Assertion Entity** — substantially-licensing-driven
  parties, per §11.7.
- **Transferee** — any subsequent owner of a Covered Patent, bound
  per §11.5.
- **Inventor veto** — named-inventor write to claims-review,
  override only by unanimous independent-board vote, per §11.10.

## 5. Why this shape — *Hypothesised on patentability strategy fit*

Five design decisions in the §3 text warrant explanation, since each
reflects a choice among real alternatives.

**Why defensive-only rather than mixed-posture with revenue
licensing.** The Lab's framework discipline asks evaluated systems to
bind their commercial leverage in ways that make accountability
possible. A lab that retained offensive-licensing options for its own
patents would be asking others to accept a discipline it refused for
itself. The meta-principle in Initiative §4 forecloses that posture
without an open contradiction.

**Why a binding-commitment-to-third-parties structure rather than
Tesla's informal blog-post pledge.** Tesla's 2014 pledge is widely
cited but is legally fragile — it depends on promissory estoppel and
has never been tested in court against a successor entity. The §11.5
transferee-binding language and §11.11 third-party-beneficiary
construction take the LOT and IPA approaches: the Pledge runs *with
the patent*, not merely with Tesla's reputation. This survives Lab
dissolution, transfer, or capture, which Tesla's pledge does not
reliably do.

**Why an inventor veto in §11.10.** Twitter's Innovator's Patent
Agreement gave each named inventor a perpetual veto on offensive
assertion of patents naming them. The veto is the structural
mechanism that prevents a future Lab leadership from quietly
weaponising patents authored by today's inventors. Without it, the
Pledge depends entirely on board discipline; with it, the people
who actually invented the technology retain a structural check. The
override condition (unanimous independent-board vote, public log) is
narrow enough that the veto is meaningful and broad enough that the
Lab is not paralysed in a genuine ecosystem emergency.

**Why the Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem scope rather than "anyone
acting in good faith."** A broader scope (covering all good-faith
implementers of any technology) would be over-promising — the Lab
cannot anticipate every domain its eventual patents might touch. A
narrower scope (only formally affiliated evaluation partners) would
exclude precisely the open ecosystem the Pledge exists to protect.
The chosen scope is the natural neighbourhood of the Lab's mission:
evaluation tooling, frameworks, and methodology published or
inspectable. The "matter of conduct, not affiliation" clause
prevents the Lab from gatekeeping the ecosystem.

**Why annual public disclosure of Covered Patents.** Disclosure is
the framework's discipline applied to the Lab's own IP behaviour.
The annual list lets external parties verify Pledge compliance,
discover prior art the Lab may have created, and assess Lab
exposure to PAEs. Without disclosure, the Pledge is unverifiable;
with disclosure, the Pledge is auditable in the same way the
Lab's published Traceability Evaluation Reports are.

## 6. Reference precedents — *Demonstrated by public record*

| Precedent | Year | Binding shape | Transferee binding | Inventor veto | Scope |
|-----------|------|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|-------|
| **Tesla "All Our Patent Are Belong To You"** | 2014 | Informal public pledge (Musk blog post) | No | No | Anyone using Tesla tech "in good faith" |
| **Twitter Innovator's Patent Agreement (IPA)** | 2012 | Binding written agreement between company and engineer | Yes — transferee must accept | **Yes** — perpetual veto | Defensive only as defined; veto on offensive use |
| **Open Invention Network (OIN)** | 2005 | Patent non-aggression community; member cross-license royalty-free | Yes — within Linux System scope | No (community model) | "Linux System" — specific list of OSS components |
| **LOT Network** | 2014 | Membership agreement; patent transfer to non-members triggers automatic license to all LOT members | Yes — by transfer trigger | No | All members, all patents on transfer |
| **Open COVID Pledge** | 2020 | Time-limited non-assertion for COVID-related uses | Limited (time-scoped) | No | COVID prevention, diagnosis, treatment, containment |
| **Google Patent Pledge to Open Source** | 2013 | Non-assertion for specific patents against OSS users | Limited | No | Named patents, against OSS users only |
| **Sundog (proposed)** | 2026+ | Binding written pledge with third-party-beneficiary construction | Yes — §11.5 | **Yes** — §11.10 with override | Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem per §11.8 |

The Sundog Pledge is closest in shape to **Twitter's IPA** (binding
+ inventor veto) layered on **LOT's transferee mechanism** (running
with the patent), scoped to a specific ecosystem the way **Open
COVID** scoped to a specific domain.

## 7. Insertion-point guidance for the Governance Policy

The §3 Pledge text is drafted as a drop-in new §11 of the
Governance Policy. This requires:

- The current §11 "Versioning and amendment" renumbers to §12.
- The cross-reference in the closing paragraph ("All claims about
  this policy are themselves subject to it.") remains in place.
- The new §11 sits between §10 board oversight (which the inventor-
  veto override mechanism in §11.10 references) and §12 versioning
  (which the Pledge-amendment procedure in §11.9 references). This
  ordering preserves the logical dependency chain.

The Initiative meta-principle (§4) does not require revision to
accommodate this clause — the Pledge operationalises that principle
for IP and does not modify it.

The IP strategy memo §10 second open issue is **resolved** by
adoption of this Pledge: it converts the open question ("should we
have one?") into a concrete proposal awaiting ratification.

## 8. Open questions for counsel-elect

These are decisions the founder cannot resolve unilaterally and that
should appear on the agenda for the elder/counsel conversation.

1. **Third-party-beneficiary enforceability.** §11.11 is intended to
   make the Pledge enforceable by every party that would otherwise
   be sued. The Tesla pledge has not been tested on this point.
   Counsel should advise on the strongest available construction in
   the Lab's jurisdiction of incorporation.

2. **Transferee-binding mechanism specifics.** §11.5 is the LOT
   model. Counsel should advise whether the strongest mechanism is
   contractual (the transfer agreement carries the Pledge text), in
   rem (the Pledge is recorded against the patent at the USPTO), or
   both. The IPA-style covenant-running-with-the-patent construction
   may be jurisdiction-specific.

3. **Inventor-veto override condition.** §11.10's "unanimous
   independent-board vote finding... no other remedy is adequate"
   may be too narrow (Lab cannot defend itself in a genuine
   emergency) or too broad (board can override veto whenever it
   wants). Counsel should advise on the specific language.

4. **Definition margin cases.** §11.7 (PAE) and §11.8 (Open AI
   Evaluation Ecosystem) both reference Lab-maintained
   interpretation notes for marginal cases. Counsel should advise on
   whether this delegation is enforceable or whether marginal
   definitions need bright-line tests in the Pledge text itself.

5. **Adoption timing.** Should the Pledge be ratified by the
   founders before incorporation and adopted formally by the board
   on incorporation, or should adoption wait until the board exists
   and is constituted? The IP memo §5 ownership-of-record reality
   suggests pre-incorporation adoption is feasible since the
   natural-person founder is the only relevant party right now;
   counsel decides.

6. **Interaction with IP assignment agreement.** The Founding Plan
   §8 IP assignment agreement transfers existing Sundog artifacts
   to the Lab on incorporation. The Pledge needs to be in effect at
   the moment of transfer so that the transferred patents (if any)
   are immediately Covered Patents. The sequencing matters; counsel
   should advise on the order of instruments.

## 9. What this Pledge does NOT do

For completeness, the Pledge does not:

- **Bind the founder pre-incorporation.** The Pledge becomes
  operative only on Lab incorporation and board ratification. Any
  patents the founder files before incorporation are not Covered
  Patents until assigned to the Lab and the Pledge is in force —
  though the IP assignment agreement (Founding Plan §8) is designed
  to make this transition seamless.
- **Foreclose all commercial activity.** The Lab may charge for
  evaluation services per Governance Policy §7. The Pledge
  forecloses *patent-based* revenue, not all revenue.
- **Make patents free.** Implementing a Covered Patent still
  requires the Lab's licence in the technical sense; the Pledge is a
  binding commitment not to *assert* the patent, not a transfer of
  ownership. This is the standard non-assertion-covenant structure.
- **Cover trademarks or copyrights.** The Pledge is patent-only. The
  Initiative copyright (Surface A per the IP memo) is governed
  separately by the Lab's choice of licence on each artifact.
- **Survive ecosystem capture.** If the Open AI Evaluation Ecosystem
  itself is captured by hostile parties (so the §11.8 definition no
  longer carves out the parties the Pledge was designed to protect),
  the §11.9 amendment procedure is the mechanism to respond. The
  Pledge cannot anticipate every adversarial reconfiguration.

## 10. Disposition note on apparatus-graphs filing candidates

The apparatus-graphs draft (§2.6 standalone-filing-candidate
ranking) identifies multiple potential filings. The Pledge applies
uniformly to every Covered Patent regardless of which apparatus
candidates ultimately get filed. This means the §6 sequencing
decision in the IP memo can proceed without re-litigating posture:
whatever counsel-elect decides to file, the Pledge governs how it
gets used. This decoupling is intentional — it lets the technical
filing question and the institutional-posture question be answered
separately.
