A significant engineering challenge faced by Sundog Software—and documented in their technical developer blogs—involves the complex mathematics of "multi-display alignment". This challenge arises when clients construct panoramic, immersive simulator displays utilizing multiple monitors, each oriented with entirely different viewing angles and projection matrices.
### The 2D Billboard Alignment Challenge
The technical difficulty centers on the rendering of specific cloud types, specifically *cumulus congestus* and the particle variants of *stratocumulus* clouds. Within the SilverLining SDK, these clouds are constructed using "2D billboard particles"—flat images that continually rotate to face the camera, creating an illusion of 3D volume while saving computational power.
When a multi-monitor display merely uses skew matrices applied to a single projection, enabling the billboard-use-ndc configuration setting within the software's resource files is sufficient to maintain visual cohesion. However, when the physical monitors point in completely different directions, the math breaks down. The 2D billboards must orient themselves differently for each specific view. If a massive cumulus cloud crosses the physical boundary between two divergently angled monitors, the billboards will conflict, leading to severe visual discontinuities and breaking the immersive simulation.
Frank Kane details the engineering compromise required to solve this: by enforcing the parameters stratocumulus-particle-billboards-per-cloud and cumulus-billboards-per-cloud to a "yes" state, the engine forces the entire cloud structure to orient in a mathematically consistent manner relative to a central viewpoint. While individual volumetric puffs of the cloud may experience slight misalignment at the micro-level, the macro-structure of the cloud remains cohesive across the screen boundaries. Alternatively, developers are advised to abandon billboard particles entirely for multi-display setups and utilize non-particle layer types like standard *stratus* or *cirrus* clouds.

### The Physics of Parhelia
A sundog, formally classified in atmospheric physics and meteorology as a *parhelion* (plural: *parhelia*), is a spectacular optical phenomenon that manifests as bright, localized spots of light in the sky, positioned on either one or both sides of the Sun.
The physical mechanism driving a sundog is pure optical refraction. The phenomenon occurs when sunlight passes through a high-altitude layer of cirrus or cirrostratus clouds located within the troposphere. These specific cloud formations are heavily populated by millions of microscopic, flat, hexagonal (six-sided) ice crystals.
As these plate-like ice crystals drift slowly downward through the atmosphere, aerodynamic drag forces them to orient themselves horizontally, functioning as a massive array of microscopic, airborne prisms. When the rays of the setting or rising sun strike the vertical side faces of these aligned crystals, the light is refracted (bent) by exactly 22 degrees. Because the refractive index of ice varies slightly depending on the wavelength of light, the light is subjected to spectral dispersion. This causes the sundog to exhibit a distinct color gradient: the edge closest to the solar disc appears red, transitioning through orange and yellow, before fading into a diffuse blue or white at the outer edge, which eventually merges into a faint, white ring known as the parhelic circle.
While they appear magical, sundogs are a relatively common and predictable occurrence in extreme winter climates. Residents of Northern Alberta, North Dakota, and Minnesota routinely document them during periods of intense cold snaps (e.g., -20°F temperatures coupled with high winds) when the requisite humidity and temperature parameters perfectly align to generate the necessary hexagonal crystals.
