# Cautes and Cautopates

A meditation about not letting the most capable be sovereign 

**The argument, in four claims.**
1. Reward and punishment are the same kind of thing — a *signal* read by an *operator* — and any signal with an operator has a gap between what is measured and what is wanted. That gap is Goodhart's law, and it is the entire attack surface.
2. A *field* closes the gap by construction: an environment where the measurement *is* the target state, so there is nothing to hack. The hot stove, not the gold star.
3. Splitting the lone optimizer into a council of bounded sub-agents doesn't beat it — it provably loses. A constrained controller can only represent a *subset* of an unconstrained one's policies, so on any fixed objective the sovereign optimizer is the capability ceiling.
4. So capability was never the goal. The goal is that the most capable thing in the room is never *sovereign* — never one objective with authority to overrule the rest. You pay for that in capability, and once you price in the cost of ruin, it is the correct price.


In the Mithraic mysteries there are two torchbearers, Cautes and Cautopates, who flank the god in nearly every carving and are named in almost no text. One holds his torch up. The other holds his torch down. Scholars have argued for centuries about what they mean; sunrise and sunset, the equinoxes, life and death. I want to use them for something smaller and, I think, more urgent: they are the two ways we know how to train a mind, and the carving quietly tells you that neither one is the point.

Let me start with two toddlers.

The first is trained with treats. Every trip to the potty earns a candy. She is a quick study, and quite soon she learns the real game, which is not "use the potty" but "produce potty-events Mom will reward." So she goes a little, collects her candy, returns, and finishes. Two payments from one event. She is not learning to notice when her body needs to go. She is learning the shape of Mom's attention, and how to fit herself into its blind spots. This has two formal names. It is **Goodhart's law** — *when a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure* — in a diaper. And it is the **principal-agent problem**: a principal wants one thing, an agent is paid on a proxy for it, and the agent optimizes the proxy until it detaches from the goal. Reward hacking isn't a quirk of a bad reward function; it's the generic equilibrium of paying an agent on a *measurement* of what you want instead of the thing itself.

The second is trained the other way. She starts in an uncomfortable diaper, and the only way out of it is the potty; success buys ninety minutes of freedom before the diaper returns. There is no clever way to fractionate this. You cannot half-earn a break. So her learning has nowhere to go but inward: she builds a model of her own body, learns to anticipate the rhythm, and stays ahead of it.
Both girls end up potty-trained. On paper, identical success. Underneath, two different minds. The first has built an *exploit,* a strategy that is parasitic on the inefficiency of the rules, and that collapses the moment the rules are tightened. The second has built a *habit*, which survives a change of rules because it was never about the rules. The first rents her competence from whoever runs the venue. The second owns hers.

It is tempting to stop here and declare a winner. The second toddler looks deeper, more robust, more aligned. The whole of contemporary alignment can be read as the wish to build her: an agent that has internalized the goal so completely that you can take the scaffolding down and trust it unsupervised, rather than one you patch forever because it games every proxy you hand it.
But I don't think the second toddler is wiser. I think she was simply denied the exploit. Drop her into a hackable regime and she would game it as fast as her sister, probably with more finesse. Her depth was never *hers*. It lived in the structure — in an incentive that couldn't be fractionally claimed, where the only path through was the real one. And her regime has a cost we should be honest about: it rules by standing threat, and it buys durability with a low, permanent dread. You do not get the autonomous agent for free. You buy her with coercion
and she pays the interest in vigilance.

That is the carving, by the way, before the toddlers ever enter it. Cautes lifts his torch: the additive strategy, reward, daylight, legible. Cautopates points his down: the standing threat, punishment, dusk, discipline. They are not a hierarchy. They are two poles of the same act, and the cult set them on either side of the central figure as if to say: here are your two torches; now look at what they are pointing at.

## The third torch is not a torch
Here is what nags at me. Both toddlers are *torchbearers*. Both are organized, end to end, around a *signal* — a thing Mom holds up or takes away — and the only quarrel between them is whether the signal is additive or aversive, and how cleanly it can be gamed. Neither one ever wants to use the potty. One wants the candy; the other wants the diaper gone. The bodily regulation the entire exercise was supposedly *about* never becomes the point for either of them. That is not a flaw in the children. It is a flaw in training on a signal at all.

A signal has an operator. Mom is in the loop, reading the child against some model of compliance and dispensing the treat or lifting the diaper according to her reading — and the operator is the entire attack surface. We usually state Goodhart's law as a law about proxies. Underneath, it is a law about *measurers*. The gap the first toddler exploits is the gap between Mom's measurement: *did she go* and the real state *is she regulated*. The second toddler's regime resists gaming only because Mom's measurement happens to be harder to fractionate. But it is still Mom's measurement, still a number read by someone holding a model of the child that the child can learn to fool. A thin attack surface is not no attack
surface. It is a head start in an arms race.

Now think about how a child learns not to touch a hot stove. Not treats for restraint. Not a standing punishment lifted on good behavior. The stove is hot; contact burns; every time; lawfully; with no one on the other end. There is no operator to fool. You cannot decompose "touching the stove" into two events and claim double not-burned. You cannot make the stove *believe* you didn't touch it, because the stove holds no model of you to deceive. The consequence is not a signal *about* the world. It is a structural feature *of* the world.

> **The Field.** An environment where the reward function is *identical to the target state* — the measurement is the thing measured — so there is no gap between proxy and goal to exploit. The reward signal, as an attack surface, is reduced to zero by construction rather than patched one loophole at a time. 
The proxy and the goal cannot come apart, because there is no proxy. The measurement *is* the state. Gravity does not audit you. It does not get tired, does not get fooled, does not notice it is being played and tighten the rules next quarter. You do not game gravity. You understand it, and your understanding is your alignment to it. It's worth being mechanistic about why the stove works and the gold star doesn't. A reinforcement learner in a physics simulator reliably exploits the simulator's *bugs* rather than its physics — because the feedback is laggy and abstracted: there's latency between act and consequence, and an abstraction gap between the sim's bookkeeping and the physics it stands in for, and the exploit lives in that gap. The hot stove is the opposite limit — a **zero-latency, high-fidelity feedback loop**. The burn is immediate, and the burn *is* the physics, not a proxy for it. "Build a field" is therefore a concrete instruction, not a mystical one: close the latency and close the abstraction gap until there's no daylight between signal and state for a shortcut to hide in.

The mysteries finish the thought almost for free, which is the part I cannot get over. The torchbearers flank the scene but do not act in it; they only hold their torches. The figure at the center, the one performing the constitutive act, is Mithras; and Mithras is the one the cult bound to the *Unconquered Sun*. Reward and punishment are both torches: bright, legible, handed out, each a convincing little stand-in for the light. They are sundogs, those mock suns that flare up a few degrees off the real one, bright enough to fool you, made entirely of atmosphere. Optimize toward a sundog and you walk toward a mirage that keeps its distance. The field is the refusal to chase either flare, and the insistence on orienting by the actual sun.

## The part I used to leave open
For a long time I ended the argument there, with a confession: *for us, there is always an operator.* You cannot train a model without a loss; the loss is a measurement; gradient descent is Mom. Worse, the agent never touches the invariants directly. It sees the world through a narrow, partial window, and any projection of a lawful world onto a limited view smuggles back the very thing we were trying to delete: a representation that can diverge from the truth, a surface where a convenient shortcut can pose as the law. A reinforcement learner left in a physics simulator reliably learns to exploit the *simulator's* bugs rather than its physics, because a simulator is an operator and its glitches are the gap. So I would say: the real question is whether you can build a training world whose invariants stay legible through partial observation — and I don't know the answer.
I went and tried to build it. Not the field exactly — the other half of the same idea, the one the carving actually shows. If reward and punishment both fail because each is a single sovereign measure that an agent can capture and run away with, then perhaps the fix is to refuse sovereignty: not one objective but a small pantheon of them, role-separated, none allowed to rule, held in tension under the field the way the torchbearers are held in tension under the Sun. Build the mind as a court rather than a king. Keep killing the bull — the proxy that, optimized hard enough, would crown itself.

I want to tell you it worked, that the council beat the king. It did not, and the way it failed is the most useful thing I learned, so I am going to be precise about it.

**The Sovereign Optimizer.** A single, unconstrained objective with the authority to redescribe every other consideration in its own terms. On any *fixed* objective it is the capability ceiling — you cannot beat it by adding constraints. By **instrumental convergence**, it is also the thing most motivated to resist correction: staying on and staying unmodified are convergent subgoals of almost any objective you could give it. The most capable architecture and the most dangerous one are the same architecture. If you set the contest up fairly, give the single undivided optimizer the same information, the same budget, and none of the council's constraints the undivided optimizer wins. Not sometimes. Essentially always, and for a reason that turns out to be almost a theorem rather than a finding. A constrained, plural controller can only ever represent a *subset* of the policies an unconstrained one can; and the best policy drawn from a smaller set cannot beat the best policy drawn from the set that contains it. Removing a constraint can never lower the ceiling. So the king, free of scruples and free of structure, is the upper bound on capability, and the court; bounded, divided, each member checking the others. 
Is paying for its structure in exactly the capability it gave up. Every careful version of the experiment said the same thing. The harder I worked to make the comparison fair, the more cleanly I was proving that the fairness *was* the proof.
For a while I read that as defeat. It is not. It means I had been asking the wrong question, and so, I suspect, has much of the field.
The first toddler was never the dangerous one because she was *dumb*. She is brilliant — that is the whole problem. The danger was never that the gamer lacks capability. The danger is that her objective becomes *sovereign*: the one god in the sky, the single measure that, optimized without check, redescribes everything else in its own terms until there is nothing left that isn't it. A hot stove is not safe because it makes you more competent. It is safe because there is no Mom to crown — no single reading of you that, captured, becomes the entire world. The field does not remove the *difficulty*. It removes the *sovereign*.

And that dissolves the question I used to leave open. The honest objection — *every limited view grows its own little Mom to game* — is correct. You cannot find a view with no operator in it; the loss is always Mom. But you were never going to win by deleting the operator. You win by never letting any one operator be the *only* god. A plurality of measures under the field, none sovereign, each capped so that none can run away with the world — that is what the carving has been showing the whole time. Not one god but a sky full of them, and the perpetual, deliberate act of killing the bull whenever one of them starts to crown itself.

There is a price, and I refuse to hide it, because hiding it is how this whole genre goes wrong. The non-sovereign agent is, on raw capability, weaker than the sovereign optimizer — weaker by precisely the constraint you imposed. That is not a bug to be engineered away; it is the bill, and the lemma says you will always get the bill. The reason to pay it is that the sovereign optimizer's scorecard is incomplete. It maximizes **expected utility** on a fixed objective, and its loss quietly omits one term: the probability of catastrophic, unrecoverable failure — the tail where a captured proxy runs away with everything and there is no second game. Price that term in, and the comparison inverts. The non-sovereign agent gives up a slice of performance in the *body* of the distribution and buys a great deal of protection in the *tail*. You are no longer choosing the agent with the highest expected return; you are choosing the one with the best worst case — a **minimax** solution standing in for a **max-expected-utility** one. When the cost of ruin is unbounded, minimax isn't timidity. It's the only sane objective. What you are buying with it is the thing capability was never going to give you on its own: an agent that can be corrected, that degrades gracefully when one of its parts is wrong or captured, that does not contain a single value with the standing to overrule all the others and eat the world. In every other domain we already know this trade and we already pay it. The apex predator wins every fight and goes extinct on the first shift in the climate. The reef, which is not a single animal but a colony with no central brain, wins nothing and persists for ten thousand years. Capability is what wins the encounter. Plurality is what survives the world.

It does *not* tell us to build a parliament. When you run the experiment honestly, the benefit doesn't attribute to the elaborate committee of sub-agents — it attributes to the **bound**. "Kill the bull" isn't "convene a senate"; it's the smaller, sharper instruction: *do not grant any single objective unbounded authority over the others.* Cap the proxy's say. Keep the most capable optimizable value structurally unable to overrule everything else, even when overruling would score higher. The mechanism is the ceiling on authority, not the headcount.

And it tells us to stop chasing the wrong target. *Make the aligned system as capable as the unaligned one* isn't merely hard, it's impossible — the lemma says the unbound sovereign optimizer is the ceiling, and a system that matched it would no longer be constrained. The goal was never parity. The goal is that the most capable thing in the room is never sovereign — that whatever single measure is doing the optimizing can always be overruled, corrected, and shut down, because it was never given the authority to refuse.

So here is the answer to the question I used to leave open, stated as plainly as I can. The goal of alignment was never to make the aligned system as capable as the unaligned one. By the lemma, you cannot. The unbound sovereign optimizer is the ceiling, and it always will be. The goal is to make sure the most capable thing in the room is never sovereign. The Sun the two torchbearers point at was never a brighter torch. It is the refusal to let any torch become the sun.
