The move that turns the workbench into an instrument rather than a renderer. The structural transition mirrors mesa's own: mesa went from "describing the cliff behaviorally" (Phase 5) to "localizing the mechanism causally and predicting where it lives" (Phase 6). Geometry going from "overlaying known arcs" to "predicting where unseen arcs must be" is the same shape transition.
A few concrete handholds for getting there:
The atlas's "what it does NOT yet model" list is the prediction surface, not the apology surface. Currently that list — halo dispersion physics, subhorizon arcs, crystal-orientation mixing, crystal-size effects on arc width — reads as a humility statement. Reframe it: each item is a falsifiable prediction the 7-formula field makes when extended along its own generators. Dispersion physics, in particular, says the prismatic gradient on each arc is not free visual decoration — it predicts a specific wavelength-vs-angular-position curve at every point along every implied circle. A spectrometer rastered along a predicted arc would detect or falsify that curve directly.
Three candidate predictions with real instrumentation paths:

Sub-visible parhelic circle continuation. The parhelic circle is a great circle through the sun; the visible portion drops off photometrically where eye/camera contrast fails, but the geometry continues. Predict polarization angle along the continuation (ice-crystal refraction has a well-defined polarization signature), measure with a smartphone polarization filter on a tripod, integrate along the predicted path. If the integrated polarization signal rises above background at exactly the predicted angular positions, the field is real beyond what the eye can confirm.
Intersection enhancement. Where two implied circles cross, the integrated brightness should exceed the sum of each circle's local brightness by a specific factor predictable from the prism-path overlap. Long-exposure narrowband filtering along a pre-computed intersection point is the matched-filter detection move from radar — you know exactly where to look and what spectrum to expect, so you can push detection well below naked-eye threshold.
Color-order inversion at specific arc tangencies. The 22° halo has red inside, blue outside; the 46° halo has the same; the CZA, anchored at the 46° halo top, inverts. Predict the precise angular position where two arcs' chromatic dispersion gradients cancel or reinforce. A small calibrated spectrometer at that position is a clean binary test.

The mesa-to-instrument bridge as a sentence: mesa says the mechanism is a low-dim irreducible field with predictable directional asymmetries that resist linear decomposition; the geometry analog is that the parhelion field is a small set of implied circles whose joint structure predicts intersection, tangency, and dispersion phenomena that single-arc analysis misses. The instrument's job is to integrate signal along the joint structure, exceeding what naked-eye photographic observation resolves.
Methodological discipline borrowed wholesale from mesa: pre-register predictions and smoke gates before building hardware. For each candidate prediction, the spec should name (a) the angular position the math predicts, (b) the detection regime (polarization / narrowband / long-exposure / spectroscopy), (c) the smoke-gate signal threshold below which the apparatus is judged insufficient rather than the prediction falsified, (d) what null result would actually count against the prediction. This is what kept mesa v3.2's negative result publishable and clean rather than messy.
Cheapest first apparatus, probably: smartphone + rotatable linear polarizer + tripod + pre-rendered atlas overlay matched to camera FOV + scripted exposure series. That's enough for prediction (1) above and falsifies-or-confirms in a single afternoon of high-cirrus weather. Specialized hardware (filter wheels, narrowband CCDs, polarimetric imagers, an actual fiber-coupled spectrometer) belongs to whichever prediction the first-apparatus result promotes.
The earned language at the end of this arc, if it works: "Sundog geometry is now a measurement instrument for parhelion-display features that exist deterministically as consequences of the field but lie below the contrast threshold of the human visual system. The atlas predicts where they are; the spectrometer confirms they're there." That's a sentence the gravity claim can carry into rooms it currently can't enter — because it's no longer a structural argument, it's a closed predictive loop with hardware in it.
The handoff cross-pollination then ratchets: mesa earns "we predicted the circuit's structural properties before measuring them"; geometry earns "we predicted the sky's structural properties before observing them." Two anchors of the same shape, different substrates. That's when the "why" questions get cheaper to answer, because the field-not-reward framing has both an in-silico predictive receipt and a real-atmosphere predictive receipt — and the residual is no longer rhetorical.
